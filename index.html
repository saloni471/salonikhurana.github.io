About

Who benefits from digital change —
and who gets left behind?

I am an Economist at the World Bank's Digital and AI Vice Presidency, Washington D.C., and a PhD Scholar in Economics at the Indian Institute of Foreign Trade (IIFT), New Delhi (thesis submitted; expected June 2026).

I study how digital skills, AI, and technological change reshape employment, wages, and inequality in developing economies — translating large-scale data into actionable evidence for policy. I also founded Data with Saloni, a mentoring initiative supporting early-career researchers on empirical methods and data analysis.

"Public Safety for Women: Is Regulation of Social Drinking Spaces Effective?"

Journal of Development Studies, 2021

with Kanika Mahajan

Exploits a natural experiment in India — a state-level crackdown on bars serving hard liquor — to estimate the causal effect of alcohol outlet regulation on crimes against women. Using difference-in-differences, finds that restricting access to hard liquor in bars reduces reported sexual assault and harassment by 25%, with no effect on reported rapes. Directly relevant for regulators designing public safety policies targeting the built environment.

"Evolution of Wage Inequality in India (1983–2017): The Role of Occupational Task Content"

Oxford University Press, 2023

with Kanika Mahajan

Documents long-run trends in wage inequality in India across four decades using National Sample Survey data. Finds that wage inequality rose between 1983 and 2004, then declined distinctly through 2018 — driven by strong wage growth at the bottom of the distribution. Attributes this reversal to shifts in the occupational task content of work, particularly the decline of routine manual tasks. No evidence of earnings polarisation. Key reading for understanding how technological change and structural transformation interact with distributional outcomes.

"Click, Code, Earn: The Returns to Digital Skills"

with Yan Liu, Antonio Martins-Neto, and Juan Porras · World Bank

Estimates wage returns to digital skills in developing economies using job postings and worker profile data. Finds that workers with verifiable digital competencies command significant wage premiums — with stronger returns in higher-income economies and among workers in formal employment. Provides a data-driven case for public investment in digital skills as a wage-boosting and inequality-reducing policy tool.

"Is Online Job Posting Data Useful in the Context of Developing Countries?"

with Shu Yu, Hieu Duc Nguyen, and Yan Liu · World Bank

Evaluates whether online job postings — increasingly used to monitor skills demand in rich economies — are valid labor market indicators in lower-income settings with lower internet penetration and informality. Finds that, while coverage is uneven, job posting data captures real-time demand signals that are not available through traditional surveys, and offers guidance on how to use these data responsibly for policy analysis in developing countries.

"Do Minimum Wages Reduce Inequality? Evidence from India"

with Kanika Mahajan and Kunal Sen

Ashoka University Discussion Paper No. 139 · Also: IZA DP 16600 · UNU-WIDER WP 2023/67

Wage inequality in India fell by 35% between 1999 and 2018, driven by strong real wage growth among low-income workers. This paper asks how much of that decline is attributable to rising minimum wages. Exploiting state-level variation in minimum wage changes — and using a border discontinuity design for robustness — it finds that a 1% increase in the minimum wage raises wages for the lowest quintile by 0.17%, with diminishing effects up the distribution. Minimum wages explain 26% of the overall decline in inequality, without adverse employment effects. Key policy implication: minimum wage policy can be an effective tool for reducing wage disparity in developing economies — including in both formal and informal sectors — without trading off jobs.

"GVCs, Skill Bias, and Wage Inequality in India"

IIFT Working Paper EC-25-77

Examines whether deeper integration into global value chains amplifies skill-biased technological change and widens wage inequality in India. Offers evidence for policymakers on how trade agreements and industrial upgrading strategies interact with distributional outcomes.
